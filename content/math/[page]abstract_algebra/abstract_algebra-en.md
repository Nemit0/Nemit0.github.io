# Abstract Algebra: Groups, Rings, and Fields

Abstract algebra studies **sets equipped with operations** that obey certain axioms. The three pillars are:

| Structure | Operations | Rough idea |
|---|---|---|
| **Group** | one ($\cdot$) | Symmetries, invertibility |
| **Ring** | two ($+$, $\cdot$) | Arithmetic without guaranteed division |
| **Field** | two ($+$, $\cdot$) | Arithmetic *with* division by every nonzero element |

Each structure builds on the previous one: every field is a ring, and the additive part of every ring is a group.

This article develops definitions, key theorems, and important examples for all three.

---

# Part I — Group Theory

## 1. Definition of a Group

> **Definition 1.1.** A **group** is a pair $(G, \cdot\,)$ where $G$ is a nonempty set and $\cdot : G \times G \to G$ is a binary operation satisfying:
>
> | Tag | Axiom | Statement |
> |---|---|---|
> | **G1** | Associativity | $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$ for all $a, b, c \in G$ |
> | **G2** | Identity | There exists $e \in G$ with $e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a$ for all $a \in G$ |
> | **G3** | Inverses | For each $a \in G$ there exists $a^{-1} \in G$ with $a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e$ |
>
> If in addition $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$ for all $a, b$, the group is **abelian** (or **commutative**).

Convention: we usually write $ab$ for $a \cdot b$ and $1$ (or $e$) for the identity.

### 1.1 First Consequences

**Proposition 1.2.** In any group:

1. The identity $e$ is unique.
2. Each element has a unique inverse.
3. $(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$ ("socks-shoes" property).
4. $(a^{-1})^{-1} = a$.
5. **Cancellation laws:** $ab = ac \Rightarrow b = c$ and $ba = ca \Rightarrow b = c$.

*Proof of (3).* $(ab)(b^{-1}a^{-1}) = a(bb^{-1})a^{-1} = aea^{-1} = aa^{-1} = e$, and similarly the other product equals $e$. $\square$

### 1.2 Notation for Powers

For $a \in G$ and $n \in \mathbb{Z}$:

$$
a^0 = e, \qquad a^n = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdots a}_{n} \; (n > 0), \qquad a^{-n} = (a^{-1})^n.
$$

The usual exponent rules hold: $a^{m+n} = a^m a^n$ and $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$.

## 2. Examples of Groups

### 2.1 Number Groups (under addition)

| Group | Set | Identity | Inverse of $a$ | Abelian? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ | Integers | $0$ | $-a$ | Yes |
| $(\mathbb{Q}, +)$ | Rationals | $0$ | $-a$ | Yes |
| $(\mathbb{R}, +)$ | Reals | $0$ | $-a$ | Yes |
| $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}, +)$ | Integers mod $n$ | $\bar 0$ | $\overline{-a}$ | Yes |

### 2.2 Multiplicative Groups

| Group | Set | Identity | Abelian? |
|---|---|---|---|
| $(\mathbb{Q}^*, \cdot\,)$ | Nonzero rationals | $1$ | Yes |
| $(\mathbb{R}^*, \cdot\,)$ | Nonzero reals | $1$ | Yes |
| $(\mathbb{C}^*, \cdot\,)$ | Nonzero complex numbers | $1$ | Yes |
| $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ | Units mod $p$ ($p$ prime) | $\bar 1$ | Yes |

### 2.3 Symmetric and Permutation Groups

The **symmetric group** $S_n$ consists of all bijections $\{1, 2, \dots, n\} \to \{1, 2, \dots, n\}$ under composition.

- $|S_n| = n!$
- $S_n$ is **non-abelian** for $n \ge 3$.
- Every permutation is a product of transpositions.
- A permutation is **even** or **odd** according to whether it decomposes into an even or odd number of transpositions.

The **alternating group** $A_n \subset S_n$ is the subgroup of even permutations, with $|A_n| = n!/2$.

### 2.4 Matrix Groups

Let $F$ be a field. The **general linear group** is:

$$
\mathrm{GL}_n(F) = \{A \in F^{n \times n} : \det A \neq 0\},
$$

under matrix multiplication. Its subgroup

$$
\mathrm{SL}_n(F) = \{A \in \mathrm{GL}_n(F) : \det A = 1\}
$$

is the **special linear group**. Both are non-abelian for $n \ge 2$.

### 2.5 Dihedral Groups

The **dihedral group** $D_n$ is the group of symmetries of a regular $n$-gon:

$$
D_n = \langle r, s \mid r^n = s^2 = e,\; srs = r^{-1} \rangle.
$$

$|D_n| = 2n$. It contains $n$ rotations and $n$ reflections. $D_n$ is non-abelian for $n \ge 3$.

### 2.6 Trivial and Cyclic Groups

- The **trivial group** $\{e\}$ has one element.
- A **cyclic group** is generated by a single element: $G = \langle g \rangle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. Every cyclic group is abelian. Up to isomorphism, the cyclic groups are $\mathbb{Z}$ (infinite) and $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (of order $n$).

## 3. Subgroups

> **Definition 3.1.** A subset $H \subseteq G$ is a **subgroup** of $G$ (written $H \le G$) if $H$ is itself a group under the same operation.

**Subgroup Test.** $\emptyset \neq H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup iff for all $a, b \in H$, $ab^{-1} \in H$.

### 3.1 Important Subgroups

- **Center:** $Z(G) = \{z \in G : zg = gz \text{ for all } g \in G\}$.
- **Centraliser:** $C_G(a) = \{g \in G : ga = ag\}$.
- **Generated subgroup:** $\langle S \rangle$ = smallest subgroup containing $S \subseteq G$.

### 3.2 Cosets and Lagrange's Theorem

For $H \le G$ and $a \in G$, the **left coset** is $aH = \{ah : h \in H\}$.

**Theorem 3.2 (Lagrange).** If $G$ is a finite group and $H \le G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$.

The integer $[G : H] = |G|/|H|$ is the **index** of $H$ in $G$.

**Corollary.** The order of every element divides $|G|$. In particular, $a^{|G|} = e$ for all $a \in G$.

### 3.3 Normal Subgroups

> **Definition 3.3.** $N \le G$ is **normal** (written $N \trianglelefteq G$) if $gNg^{-1} = N$ for all $g \in G$, i.e., $gng^{-1} \in N$ for every $n \in N$ and $g \in G$.

Equivalently, $N$ is normal iff $gN = Ng$ for all $g$.

- Every subgroup of an abelian group is normal.
- $\{e\}$ and $G$ are always normal in $G$.
- $A_n \trianglelefteq S_n$ for all $n$.

## 4. Homomorphisms and Isomorphisms

> **Definition 4.1.** A function $\varphi : G \to H$ between groups is a **homomorphism** if
> $$\varphi(ab) = \varphi(a)\varphi(b) \quad \text{for all } a, b \in G.$$

Properties:

1. $\varphi(e_G) = e_H$.
2. $\varphi(a^{-1}) = \varphi(a)^{-1}$.
3. $\varphi(a^n) = \varphi(a)^n$ for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

### 4.1 Kernel and Image

- **Kernel:** $\ker \varphi = \{a \in G : \varphi(a) = e_H\} \trianglelefteq G$.
- **Image:** $\operatorname{im} \varphi = \{\varphi(a) : a \in G\} \le H$.

$\varphi$ is injective iff $\ker \varphi = \{e\}$.

### 4.2 Isomorphisms

A **bijective homomorphism** is an **isomorphism**. We write $G \cong H$.

**Examples:**

- $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \cong \{1, -1\}$ (under multiplication).
- $(\mathbb{R}, +) \cong (\mathbb{R}^{>0}, \cdot\,)$ via $\varphi(x) = e^x$.

### 4.3 Quotient Groups

If $N \trianglelefteq G$, the set of cosets $G/N = \{gN : g \in G\}$ forms a group under

$$
(aN)(bN) = (ab)N.
$$

Normality of $N$ is precisely what makes this well-defined.

### 4.4 The First Isomorphism Theorem

**Theorem 4.2 (First Isomorphism Theorem).** If $\varphi : G \to H$ is a homomorphism, then

$$
G / \ker\varphi \;\cong\; \operatorname{im}\varphi.
$$

This is arguably the most important theorem in group theory. It says every homomorphism factors as a surjection onto a quotient followed by an injection.

### 4.5 Second and Third Isomorphism Theorems

**Second Isomorphism Theorem.** If $H \le G$ and $N \trianglelefteq G$, then $HN/N \cong H/(H \cap N)$.

**Third Isomorphism Theorem.** If $N \subseteq M$ are both normal in $G$, then $(G/N)/(M/N) \cong G/M$.

## 5. Structure Theorems for Groups

### 5.1 Fundamental Theorem of Finite Abelian Groups

**Theorem 5.1.** Every finite abelian group is isomorphic to a direct product of cyclic groups of prime-power order:

$$
G \cong \mathbb{Z}/p_1^{a_1}\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/p_2^{a_2}\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/p_k^{a_k}\mathbb{Z}.
$$

This decomposition is unique up to reordering of factors.

**Example.** The abelian groups of order $12$ are (up to isomorphism):

- $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$
- $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$  (equivalently $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$)

### 5.2 Cayley's Theorem

**Theorem 5.2.** Every group $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of a symmetric group. Specifically, $G \hookrightarrow S_G$ via the left-regular representation $g \mapsto \lambda_g$, where $\lambda_g(x) = gx$.

If $|G| = n$, then $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_n$.

### 5.3 Sylow Theorems

Let $G$ be a finite group with $|G| = p^a m$ where $p \nmid m$.

**Sylow's Theorems:**

1. **Existence.** $G$ has a subgroup of order $p^a$ (called a **Sylow $p$-subgroup**).
2. **Conjugacy.** Any two Sylow $p$-subgroups are conjugate in $G$.
3. **Counting.** The number $n_p$ of Sylow $p$-subgroups satisfies $n_p \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ and $n_p \mid m$.

The Sylow theorems are the fundamental tool for analysing the structure of finite groups.

# Part II — Ring Theory

## 6. Definition of a Ring

> **Definition 6.1.** A **ring** is a triple $(R, +, \cdot\,)$ where:
>
> 1. $(R, +)$ is an abelian group with identity $0$.
> 2. Multiplication $\cdot$ is associative: $(ab)c = a(bc)$.
> 3. Distributivity holds on both sides:
>    $$a(b + c) = ab + ac, \qquad (a + b)c = ac + bc.$$

A ring is:

| Property | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Commutative** | $ab = ba$ for all $a, b \in R$ |
| **With unity (unital)** | There exists $1 \in R$ with $1 \cdot a = a \cdot 1 = a$ for all $a$ |
| **Division ring** | Every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse |

> **Convention.** In this article, all rings are assumed to be **commutative with unity** unless stated otherwise.

### 6.1 Immediate Consequences

**Proposition 6.2.** In any ring:

1. $0 \cdot a = a \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $a$.
2. $(-a)b = a(-b) = -(ab)$.
3. $(-a)(-b) = ab$.
4. If $R$ has unity, $(-1)a = -a$.
5. The unity $1$ (if it exists) is unique.

### 6.2 Units

An element $a \in R$ is a **unit** if there exists $b \in R$ with $ab = ba = 1$. The set of units forms a group under multiplication, denoted $R^*$ or $R^\times$.

### 6.3 Zero Divisors

A nonzero element $a \in R$ is a **zero divisor** if there exists a nonzero $b \in R$ with $ab = 0$.

> **Definition 6.3.** A commutative ring with unity having **no zero divisors** is called an **integral domain**.

In an integral domain the cancellation law holds: $ab = ac$ and $a \neq 0$ imply $b = c$.

---

## 7. Examples of Rings

| Ring | Commutative? | Unity? | Integral Domain? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | Yes | Yes ($1$) | Yes | Prototype of a ring |
| $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ | Yes | Yes ($\bar 1$) | Iff $n$ is prime | $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$: $\bar 2 \cdot \bar 3 = \bar 0$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}[x]$, $\mathbb{R}[x]$, $F[x]$ | Yes | Yes ($1$) | Yes | Polynomial ring |
| $M_n(F)$ ($n \ge 2$) | No | Yes ($I_n$) | No | Matrix ring |
| $\mathbb{Z}[i] = \{a + bi : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ | Yes | Yes ($1$) | Yes | Gaussian integers |
| $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ | Yes | Yes ($1$) | Yes | Not a UFD |
| $2\mathbb{Z}$ | Yes | No | — | Even integers |
| $C([0,1], \mathbb{R})$ | Yes | Yes | No | Ring of continuous functions |

## 8. Ideals

> **Definition 8.1.** A subset $I \subseteq R$ is an **ideal** (written $I \trianglelefteq R$) if:
> 1. $(I, +)$ is a subgroup of $(R, +)$.
> 2. For all $r \in R$ and $a \in I$, $ra \in I$ and $ar \in I$ (**absorption**).

For commutative rings, conditions simplify to: $I$ is an additive subgroup and $ra \in I$ for all $r \in R$, $a \in I$.

### 8.1 Principal Ideals

The ideal **generated by** $a \in R$ is $(a) = aR = \{ar : r \in R\}$.

A ring in which every ideal is principal is a **principal ideal domain** (PID).

**Examples of PIDs:** $\mathbb{Z}$, $F[x]$ (for any field $F$).

### 8.2 Prime and Maximal Ideals

> **Definition 8.2.** A proper ideal $P \subsetneq R$ is:
> - **Prime** if $ab \in P \Rightarrow a \in P$ or $b \in P$.
> - **Maximal** if there is no ideal $I$ with $P \subsetneq I \subsetneq R$.

**Theorem 8.3.** Every maximal ideal is prime. (The converse is false in general.)

**Key correspondence:**

| Ideal type | Quotient ring property |
|---|---|
| $I$ is **prime** | $R/I$ is an **integral domain** |
| $I$ is **maximal** | $R/I$ is a **field** |

This is a central result connecting ideals and quotient rings.

**Example.** In $\mathbb{Z}$:

- $(p)$ is maximal (and prime) for every prime $p$, since $\mathbb{Z}/(p) \cong \mathbb{F}_p$ is a field.
- $(0)$ is prime (since $\mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain) but not maximal.

---

## 9. Ring Homomorphisms

> **Definition 9.1.** A function $\varphi : R \to S$ between rings is a **ring homomorphism** if:
> 1. $\varphi(a + b) = \varphi(a) + \varphi(b)$,
> 2. $\varphi(ab) = \varphi(a)\varphi(b)$,
> 3. $\varphi(1_R) = 1_S$ (when both rings have unity).

- $\ker \varphi = \{a \in R : \varphi(a) = 0\}$ is an ideal of $R$.
- $\operatorname{im} \varphi$ is a subring of $S$.

### 9.1 Quotient Rings

For $I \trianglelefteq R$, the quotient $R/I = \{a + I : a \in R\}$ is a ring with operations:

$$
(a + I) + (b + I) = (a + b) + I, \qquad (a + I)(b + I) = ab + I.
$$

**Theorem 9.2 (First Isomorphism Theorem for Rings).** If $\varphi : R \to S$ is a ring homomorphism, then

$$
R / \ker\varphi \;\cong\; \operatorname{im}\varphi.
$$

## 10. Special Classes of Rings

The algebraic hierarchy of commutative rings is:

$$
\text{Fields} \;\subset\; \text{Euclidean Domains} \;\subset\; \text{PIDs} \;\subset\; \text{UFDs} \;\subset\; \text{Integral Domains} \;\subset\; \text{Commutative Rings}
$$

### 10.1 Euclidean Domains

A **Euclidean domain** is an integral domain $R$ with a **Euclidean function** $\delta : R \setminus \{0\} \to \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ such that for all $a, b \in R$ with $b \neq 0$, there exist $q, r \in R$ with

$$
a = bq + r, \quad \text{where } r = 0 \text{ or } \delta(r) < \delta(b).
$$

**Examples:** $\mathbb{Z}$ (with $\delta(n) = |n|$), $F[x]$ (with $\delta(f) = \deg f$), $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ (with $\delta(a + bi) = a^2 + b^2$).

### 10.2 Principal Ideal Domains (PIDs)

Every Euclidean domain is a PID, but the converse fails. The classic counterexample is $\mathbb{Z}\!\left[\frac{1 + \sqrt{-19}}{2}\right]$, which is a PID but not Euclidean.

**Key property:** In a PID, every nonzero prime ideal is maximal.

### 10.3 Unique Factorisation Domains (UFDs)

An integral domain is a **UFD** if every nonzero non-unit can be written as a finite product of irreducibles, and this factorisation is unique up to order and associates.

**Theorem 10.1.** Every PID is a UFD.

The converse is false: $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is a UFD but not a PID (the ideal $(2, x)$ is not principal).

**Non-example of a UFD:** $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$, where $6 = 2 \cdot 3 = (1 + \sqrt{-5})(1 - \sqrt{-5})$ gives two distinct irreducible factorisations.

### 10.4 Polynomial Rings

**Theorem 10.2.** If $R$ is a UFD, then $R[x]$ is a UFD. (Gauss's Lemma + content arguments.)

Hence $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, $\mathbb{Z}[x, y]$, $F[x_1, \dots, x_n]$, etc. are all UFDs.

**Theorem 10.3 (Hilbert's Basis Theorem).** If $R$ is Noetherian, then $R[x]$ is Noetherian.

# Part III — Field Theory

## 11. Definition and Basic Properties

> **Definition 11.1.** A **field** is a commutative ring with unity in which every nonzero element is a unit. Equivalently, $(F, +, \cdot\,)$ is a field iff:
> 1. $(F, +)$ is an abelian group with identity $0$,
> 2. $(F \setminus \{0\}, \cdot\,)$ is an abelian group with identity $1$,
> 3. $a(b + c) = ab + ac$ for all $a, b, c \in F$.

**Proposition 11.2.** Every field is an integral domain.

*Proof.* Suppose $ab = 0$ and $a \neq 0$. Then $b = a^{-1}(ab) = a^{-1} \cdot 0 = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 11.3.** Every finite integral domain is a field.

*Proof.* Let $|R| = n$ and $a \neq 0$. The map $R \to R$ given by $x \mapsto ax$ is injective (by cancellation), hence surjective (since $R$ is finite). So $ax = 1$ for some $x$. $\square$

### 11.1 Characteristic

The **characteristic** of a field $F$, denoted $\operatorname{char} F$, is the smallest positive integer $p$ such that
$$\underbrace{1 + 1 + \cdots + 1}_{p} = 0,$$
or $0$ if no such $p$ exists.

**Theorem 11.4.** The characteristic of a field is either $0$ or a prime.

*Proof.* If $\operatorname{char} F = n = ab$ with $1 < a, b < n$, then $(\underbrace{1+\cdots+1}_a)(\underbrace{1+\cdots+1}_b) = 0$, so one factor is $0$, contradicting the minimality of $n$. $\square$

### 11.2 Prime Subfield

Every field $F$ contains a unique smallest subfield, called the **prime subfield**:

- If $\operatorname{char} F = 0$, the prime subfield is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Q}$.
- If $\operatorname{char} F = p$, the prime subfield is isomorphic to $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$.

---

## 12. Field Extensions

> **Definition 12.1.** If $F \subseteq E$ are fields (sharing the same operations), $E$ is a **field extension** of $F$, written $E/F$. (This is *not* a quotient.)

$E$ is then a **vector space over $F$**, and the dimension $\dim_F E = [E : F]$ is the **degree** of the extension.

### 12.1 Algebraic and Transcendental Elements

An element $\alpha \in E$ is:

- **Algebraic** over $F$ if $f(\alpha) = 0$ for some nonzero $f \in F[x]$.
- **Transcendental** over $F$ if no such polynomial exists.

**Examples:**

- $\sqrt{2}$ is algebraic over $\mathbb{Q}$ (root of $x^2 - 2$).
- $\pi$ and $e$ are transcendental over $\mathbb{Q}$ (proved by Lindemann and Hermite).

### 12.2 Minimal Polynomial

If $\alpha$ is algebraic over $F$, the **minimal polynomial** $m_\alpha(x) \in F[x]$ is the unique monic irreducible polynomial with $m_\alpha(\alpha) = 0$.

**Theorem 12.2.** $F(\alpha) \cong F[x]/(m_\alpha(x))$, and $[F(\alpha) : F] = \deg m_\alpha$.

### 12.3 Degree Formula (Tower Law)

**Theorem 12.3.** If $F \subseteq K \subseteq E$ are fields, then

$$
[E : F] = [E : K] \cdot [K : F].
$$

This is the key computational tool for degrees. For example:

$$
[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}] = [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2 \cdot 2 = 4.
$$

### 12.4 Algebraic Extensions

An extension $E/F$ is **algebraic** if every element of $E$ is algebraic over $F$.

**Theorem 12.4.** Every finite extension is algebraic. (The converse is false: the algebraic closure $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}$ is algebraic but infinite-dimensional.)

---

## 13. Splitting Fields and Algebraic Closure

### 13.1 Splitting Fields

A polynomial $f \in F[x]$ **splits** over $E$ if $f$ factors into linear factors in $E[x]$:

$$
f(x) = c(x - \alpha_1)(x - \alpha_2) \cdots (x - \alpha_n), \quad \alpha_i \in E.
$$

The **splitting field** of $f$ over $F$ is the smallest extension of $F$ over which $f$ splits.

**Theorem 13.1.** Splitting fields exist and are unique up to $F$-isomorphism.

**Example.** The splitting field of $x^2 + 1$ over $\mathbb{R}$ is $\mathbb{C} = \mathbb{R}(i)$.

### 13.2 Algebraic Closure

> **Definition 13.2.** A field $\overline{F}$ is an **algebraic closure** of $F$ if:
> 1. $\overline{F}/F$ is algebraic, and
> 2. $\overline{F}$ is algebraically closed (every nonconstant polynomial in $\overline{F}[x]$ has a root in $\overline{F}$).

**Theorem 13.3 (Existence and Uniqueness).** Every field has an algebraic closure, unique up to $F$-isomorphism. (Existence uses Zorn's lemma.)

**Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.** $\mathbb{C}$ is algebraically closed, i.e., $\overline{\mathbb{R}} = \mathbb{C}$.

---

## 14. Finite Fields

### 14.1 Classification

**Theorem 14.1.** For every prime power $q = p^k$, there exists a unique (up to isomorphism) field with $q$ elements, denoted $\mathbb{F}_q$ or $\mathrm{GF}(q)$.

Moreover:

1. $\mathbb{F}_q$ is the splitting field of $x^q - x$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$.
2. $\mathbb{F}_q^* = \mathbb{F}_q \setminus \{0\}$ is a **cyclic group** of order $q - 1$.
3. $\mathbb{F}_{p^m} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ iff $m \mid n$.

### 14.2 Construction of $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$

To build $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$, find an irreducible polynomial $f(x)$ of degree $k$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, then:

$$
\mathbb{F}_{p^k} \cong \mathbb{F}_p[x] / (f(x)).
$$

**Example: $\mathbb{F}_4$.** Take $f(x) = x^2 + x + 1$, which is irreducible over $\mathbb{F}_2$. Then:

$$
\mathbb{F}_4 = \mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^2 + x + 1) = \{0, 1, \alpha, \alpha + 1\},
$$

where $\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$ (since $\alpha^2 + \alpha + 1 = 0$ in characteristic $2$).

### 14.3 Frobenius Endomorphism

The map $\varphi : \mathbb{F}_q \to \mathbb{F}_q$ defined by $\varphi(a) = a^p$ is a field automorphism called the **Frobenius endomorphism**. The Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p)$ is cyclic of order $n$, generated by $\varphi$.

## 15. Galois Theory (Overview)

Galois theory establishes a deep connection between field extensions and group theory.

### 15.1 Automorphisms

An **automorphism** of $E/F$ is a field isomorphism $\sigma : E \to E$ fixing $F$ pointwise ($\sigma(a) = a$ for all $a \in F$).

The set of all such automorphisms forms a group under composition, the **Galois group** $\mathrm{Gal}(E/F)$.

### 15.2 Galois Extensions

An algebraic extension $E/F$ is **Galois** if $|\mathrm{Gal}(E/F)| = [E : F]$.

Equivalently, $E/F$ is Galois iff $E$ is the splitting field of a separable polynomial over $F$.

### 15.3 The Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

**Theorem 15.1.** Let $E/F$ be a finite Galois extension with Galois group $G = \mathrm{Gal}(E/F)$. There is an inclusion-reversing bijection:

$$
\{\text{intermediate fields } F \subseteq K \subseteq E\} \;\longleftrightarrow\; \{\text{subgroups } H \le G\}
$$

given by:

$$
K \mapsto \mathrm{Gal}(E/K), \qquad H \mapsto E^H = \{a \in E : \sigma(a) = a \;\forall\, \sigma \in H\}.
$$

Moreover:

| Field side | Group side |
|---|---|
| $[E : K]$ | $|H|$ |
| $[K : F]$ | $[G : H]$ |
| $K/F$ is Galois | $H \trianglelefteq G$ |
| $\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)$ | $G/H$ (when $H \trianglelefteq G$) |

### 15.4 Applications of Galois Theory

- **Solvability by radicals.** A polynomial $f \in F[x]$ is solvable by radicals iff $\mathrm{Gal}(E/F)$ is a solvable group. Since $S_5$ is not solvable, the **general quintic is not solvable by radicals** (Abel–Ruffini theorem).
- **Impossibility of classical constructions.** Using Galois theory one proves that it is impossible to:
  - Trisect a general angle with compass and straightedge,
  - Double the cube,
  - Square the circle (this also requires the transcendence of $\pi$).

## 16. Summary: The Algebraic Hierarchy

$$
\boxed{
\text{Group} \;\xrightarrow{+\text{ second op.}}\; \text{Ring} \;\xrightarrow{+\text{ inverses}}\; \text{Field}
}
$$

| Concept | Group | Ring | Field |
|---|---|---|---|
